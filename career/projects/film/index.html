This is a student short film produced together with Tobias Burghardt as final course project for the seminar Audiovisual Media I (Video editing and camera technique) during my Master studies at the University of TÃ¼bingen.

For both of us without any previous filmmaking experience, this was our very first time writing a script, creating a shot list, directing, acting, as well as editing a film. During the process of learning all of these skills from the ground up, we collected much valuable experience about how to plan, produce, edit and act in any film project.

Tobias Burghardt and I completed this project together in a joint team effort. Due to covid restrictions we worked with a DSLR camera and unfortunately could not have any additional people on set. Therefore we had to plan each shot together beforehand, self-directing while acting or operating the camera. You might notice that any shot with both of us in it is recorded as a static shot or has its camera translation added in post production.

We shared the workload equally during this project, the only exception to that are the text / graphic interface overlays created completely by Tobias and sound design being my part. You can read more details about creating the sound scape in post production here.

Timeline explanation / sound design

The film's timeline contains the following distinct tracks:

  • Three source video tracks for arranging the video material in a meaningful manner (especially important to keep timing with cutaways)
  • A video track for generated text overlays, such as the title, phone message overlay and credits
  • Three source audio tracks that belong to their respective video input. These contain the originally captured sound
  • A muted audio track, used to quickly disable the captured audio of any shot without deleting it from the timeline
  • Radio / Phone ringing is used for the story-critical digital sounds that the protagonist hears himself
  • Audio track roomtone / forest to keep a continuous sound ambiance of the environment without harsh cuts in the audio when the shot changes
  • Infrasound at 10 Hz aims to further a slowly growing unconscious uneasiness in the viewer - most useful when viewing the film with high-end audio equipment
  • Happy music is used to set a good, careless mood in the first half of the film and during the credits
  • Two tracks for different horror music, first only as a slight premonition to communicate the emotional distress of the protagonist, later stronger and laid on top of each other to convey his panic
  • SFX atmospheric are any ethereal elements which are laid on top of any horror music to create the final soundscape
  • SFX Musical contains all sound effects that have a certain tonality to them
  • SFX heartbeat plays a rapidly beating heart during the running scene, creating tension and greater emotional connection of the viewer to the protagonist
  • SFX panic are any audio clips designed to create dread and fear in the most chilling moments
  • SFX Sounds collects all miscellaneous sound effects, like horror stingers or sounds that reflect the action shown in the video
